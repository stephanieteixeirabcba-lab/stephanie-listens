the real problem

Why "follow the plan" isn't always enough.

Behavior plans are essential — but they can't script every moment. When a session stops working, "follow the plan" doesn't tell you whether the plan still fits the moment in front of you, whether something has changed in the environment, or when a situation has moved beyond what you should handle alone.

The gap isn't effort or care. It's not having a structured way to slow down, notice what's actually happening, and choose a next step that stays within your role. That's exactly what the LISTEN Reset gives you.

the framework

The six LISTEN Reset steps.

L

Look

Notice what is actually happening right now — behavior, body, environment — before interpreting it.

I

Identify

Separate observation from interpretation. State what you saw, not what you assumed it meant.

S

Scan

Check the context: safety, communication, environmental changes, and whether this is within your scope.

T

Think

Weigh the four options — continue, adapt, pause, or contact your supervisor — against what you observed.

E

Enact

Choose the safest, most appropriate within-scope next step and follow through calmly.

N

Notify

Communicate clearly with your BCBA using objective language and the information they need to act.

how it's built

Evidence-informed, honestly framed.

The LISTEN Reset draws on established principles from behavior analysis and compassionate, person-centered practice. It is designed to support — not override — your clinical judgment and supervision.

It is described as evidence-informed rather than a validated protocol. It's a structured decision aid, not a treatment that has been tested for clinical outcomes.

tested with real practitioners

Shaped by a formative pilot.

Before release, the kit was piloted with practicing BTs and BCBAs in a formative usability and social-validity evaluation. Testers used the tools on realistic, fully deidentified scenarios and told us what was clear, what was confusing, and what needed to change.

Their feedback drove real revisions to the wording, structure, and worksheets. This was a product-improvement pilot focused on usability and professional relevance — not a study of clinical effectiveness.
